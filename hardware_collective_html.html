HARDWARE COLLECTIVE

We meet once a month in Bangalore to build things that probably shouldn't exist but absolutely need to. Open hardware. Shared schematics. No gatekeeping. Just people who care about making stuff work.

WHAT WE DO

Bring your half-finished project. That board that won't boot. The idea that seems impossible. We debug together, share war stories, pass around firmware like folk songs. Sometimes things work. Sometimes they catch fire. Always we learn.

WHO HOSTS

Amit started this whole thing. He's been taking apart electronics since childhood—radios, computers, anything with screws. The goal was never fixing. It was understanding how the pieces talked to each other.

FULL STACK

He builds everything. Frontend, backend, hardware, firmware. That's what happens when you're making something and there's nobody else to hand it off to. You figure it out or it doesn't get built.

WHY THIS EXISTS

Hardware is hard. You need people who get it. Who understand that "works on my bench" isn't shipping. Who know the supply chain will betray you. Who've been there at 3am when nothing makes sense.

ABSURD
ABSURD INDUSTRIES

Amit runs Absurd Industries. The name comes from Camus, not comedy. They make keyboards with AI, hardware you can modify, everything GPL licensed. Open systems are more interesting than closed ones, so why build walls?

THE REALITY

Prototype three just fried. The parts you need are backordered for months. Your timeline was optimistic to begin with. You order alternatives and redesign around what's actually available. This is the work.

TOGETHER

Someone solved your exact power regulation problem last month. You help them debug their SPI communication. Schematics get forked and improved. This is how things actually move forward.

WHAT HAPPENS

The boards ship. Someone across the world forks your design, makes it better, sends back improvements. What you built six months ago is teaching someone today. That's the whole point of open hardware.

THE OBSESSION

You wake up thinking about trace impedance. You sketch circuits on napkins. You read datasheets for entertainment. This probably isn't healthy but you're not stopping and neither are we.
